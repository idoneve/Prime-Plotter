\begin{abstract}
This implements a parallel segmented sieve of Eratosthenes in C to generate primes 
efficiently, benchmark process-level and thread-level parallelism, empirically 
verify the Prime Number Theorem, and visualize prime distributions through the
Ulam spiral. The parallel implementation achieves about a 380\% speedup with 64 workers before overhead dominates. The empirical prime density matches the theoretical prediction $1/\ln(x)$ across ranges up to $8.0\times10^9$ (Took the sieve around two and half minutes to compute)
\end{abstract}

% ============================================================
\section{Introduction}

Prime numbers (integers greater than 1 divisible only by 1 and themselves) are the fundamental building blocks of the integers. Their distribution has fascinated mathematicians for millennia, from Euclid's proof of their infinitude to Riemann's still-unproven hypothesis about the zeros of the zeta function.

This paper explores three interconnected aspects of prime numbers: efficient computation via sieving algorithms, empirical verification of the Prime Number Theorem, and visual exploration of hidden structure through the Ulam spiral. It implements a parallel segmented sieve in C\footnote{Full source code and benchmarks available at \url{https://github.com/idoneve/Prime-Plotter}.}, benchmark its scaling across multiple processes and threads, and generate visualizations that confirm theoretical predictions about prime density and gap distributions.

\section{The Sieve of Eratosthenes}

The sieve of Eratosthenes is one of the oldest algorithms for finding all primes up to a given limit $n$. The algorithm proceeds as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Write the integers $2, 3, 4, \ldots, n$.
    \item Let $p = 2$, the smallest unmarked number, it is prime.
    \item Cross off all multiples of $p$ greater than $p$.
    \item Find the next unmarked number and set $p$ to it. Repeat from step 3.
    \item Stop when $p > \sqrt{n}$.
\end{enumerate}

After the algorithm terminates, all unmarked numbers are prime. The only optimization needed is that once $p > \sqrt{n}$, any remaining composite must have a prime factor $\leq \sqrt{n}$, so all numbers have already been crossed off.

Figure \ref{fig:sieve} visualizes the sieve at each prime step for $n = 100$. Black squares represent unmarked (candidate prime) numbers, while gray squares have been eliminated as composite.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{sieve_visual.png}
    \caption{The sieve of Eratosthenes for $n=100$, shown after sieving multiples 
    of each prime $p \leq \sqrt{100}$. The final row shows all 25 primes $\leq 100$.}
    \label{fig:sieve}
\end{figure}

The complexity of this algorithm is $O(n \log \log n)$, making it nearly linear. For the computations, it uses a \textit{segmented} variant that sieves the range in blocks, reducing memory usage and enabling parallelization. We delegate the actual sieving to the highly-optimized \texttt{primesieve} library \cite{primesieve}.

\section{Parallel Implementation}

Generating primes up to $8.0\times10^9$ requires sieving eight billion numbers, a task well-suited to parallel computation. The C implementation distributes work across multiple \textit{processes} (via \texttt{fork()}) and multiple \textit{threads} per process (via \texttt{pthreads}).

\subsection{Design}

The number line is divided into equal segments. Each process is assigned a contiguous range, and within each process, threads subdivide that range further. Each thread independently sieves its segment using \texttt{primesieve} and writes results to a separate CSV file. Because segments are disjoint, no synchronization or communication between workers is needed, so this is an \textit{embarrassingly parallel} workload.

\subsection{Processes vs.\ Threads}

Processes and threads represent two fundamental approaches to parallelism:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Processes} have separate memory spaces. They avoid cache contention and lock overhead, but have higher creation cost.\item \textbf{Threads} share memory within a process. They are cheaper to create, but compete for caches and memory bandwidth, limiting scaling.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{minipage}{0.9\textwidth}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{parallel_analysis.png}
        \caption{Parallel scaling benchmarks.}
        \label{fig:parallel}
        
        \vspace{0.3cm}
        \raggedright
        \textbf{(Left)} Wall time decreases from 5.5s (1 worker) to 1.5s (64 workers), then increases to 1.6s at 256 workers as process creation overhead dominates.
        
        \textbf{(Center)} Speedup (log-log scale). Ideal linear scaling is shown dashed; actual speedup plateaus at 3.8$\times$.

    \end{minipage}

Figure \ref{fig:parallel} summarizes the benchmark results. The sweet spot occurs at 64 workers (8 processes $\times$ 8 threads), achieving a 3.8 times speedup. Beyond this, each worker's segment becomes too small, at 256 workers, each chunk is only $\sim$39,000 numbers, and the overhead of spawning processes and threads outweighs the benefit of further subdivision.

\section{The Prime Number Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Prime Number Theorem]
    Let $\pi(x)$ denote the number of primes $\leq x$. Then \[\pi(x) \sim \frac{x}{\ln x}\] as $x \to \infty$. Equivalently, the density of primes near $x$ is approximately $1/\ln(x)$, and the average gap between consecutive primes near $x$ is approximately $\ln(x)$.
\end{theorem}

Using the parallel sieve, we computed primes up to $8.0\times10^9$ and verified the PNT empirically. Figure \ref{fig:pnt} shows the results.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{subfigure}{0.48\textwidth}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{pnt_density.png}
        \caption{Prime density vs.\ $x$. Red dots are computed densities; the blue curve is $1/\ln(x)$.}
        \label{fig:pnt_density}
    \end{subfigure}
    \hfill
    \begin{subfigure}{0.48\textwidth}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{pnt_gaps.png}
        \caption{Average prime gap vs.\ $x$. Red dots are computed averages; the blue curve is $\ln(x)$.}
        \label{fig:pnt_gaps}

    \caption{Empirical verification of the Prime Number Theorem. Both density and average gap closely follow theoretical predictions.}
    \label{fig:pnt}

The close agreement between computed data (red points) and theoretical curves (blue lines) confirms the PNT across the range $1$ to $8.0\times10^9$. The density decays from approximately 5.2\% near $2.4 \times 10^8$ to 4.6\% near $2.6\times10^9$, matching the logarithmic decay predicted by theory.

\section{Prime Gaps}

The difference $g_n = p_{n+1} - p_n$ between consecutive primes is called a prime gap. Figure \ref{fig:gaps} shows the distribution and behavior of prime gaps in the computed range. In which twin primes constitute approximately 0.28--0.36\% of gaps, with the fraction decreasing as predicted. The maximum observed gap was around 350, consistent with Cram\'er's conjecture that maximal gaps grow like $\ln^2(p_n)$.

\section{The Ulam Spiral}
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{ulam_spiral.png}
    \caption{Ulam spiral of primes up to $8.0\times10^9$ (Feel free to zoom in as much as you want). Black dots are primes; diagonal striations correspond to prime-generating quadratic polynomials such as Euler's $n^2 + n + 41$.}
    \label{fig:ulam}
\end{figure}

\begin{remark}
    In 1963, Stanis\l{}aw Ulam discovered a striking visual pattern by arranging 
    numbers in a square spiral and marking the primes. Diagonal lines emerge, these 
    correspond to quadratic polynomials that produce unusually many primes.
    The most famous diagonal corresponds to Euler's polynomial $n^2 + n + 41$, which produces primes for $n = 0, 1, \ldots, 39$. Other diagonals correspond to polynomials like $4n^2 + 2n + 41$ and $4n^2 + 4n + 59$. The existence of such 
    prime-rich quadratics is connected to class numbers of imaginary quadratic fields, but a complete explanation remains an open problem in number theory.\newline
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
    The Ulam spiral demonstrates that prime numbers, despite their apparent 
    randomness, possess subtle structure. It is a powerful reminder that visualization can reveal patterns invisible to purely analytic approaches.
\end{remark}

\section{Conclusion}

This project demonstrates the interplay between theory and computation in number 
theory. The parallel segmented sieve efficiently generates primes up to $8.0\times10^9$, 
enabling empirical verification of the Prime Number Theorem and exploration of 
prime gaps. The Ulam spiral visualization reveals hidden quadratic structure in 
prime distributions.

Key findings include: (1) parallel speedup reaches 3.8$\times$ at 64 workers 
before overhead dominates, (2) empirical prime density matches $1/\ln(x)$ with 
high precision, and (3) diagonal patterns in the Ulam spiral persist at large 
scales, reflecting deep connections between primes and quadratic polynomials.
